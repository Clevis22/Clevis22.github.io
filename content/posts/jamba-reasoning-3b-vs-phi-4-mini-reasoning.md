Jamba Reasoning 3B vs Phi-4-mini-reasoning is the most interesting head-to-head in the small reasoning model tier right now, because the two models represent opposite bets on what a sub-4B reasoning model should be. AI21 built Jamba Reasoning 3B as a general reasoner with a 256K context window and a hybrid Mamba/Transformer architecture that stays fast as the prompt grows. Microsoft built Phi-4-mini-reasoning as a math specialist, a dense 3.8B transformer fine-tuned on 150 billion tokens of synthetic math that outscores 7B models on competition benchmarks. Both fit comfortably on a laptop. This post compares the specs, the benchmark stories each vendor tells, and the practical differences you hit when running them locally.

We have covered both models individually: the [Jamba Reasoning 3B local setup guide](/posts/run-jamba-reasoning-3b-locally/) and the [Phi-4-mini-reasoning local setup guide](/posts/run-phi-4-mini-reasoning-locally/) go deeper on deployment. This post is about choosing between them.

## Jamba Reasoning 3B vs Phi-4-mini-reasoning: specs at a glance

| Spec | Jamba Reasoning 3B | Phi-4-mini-reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Developer | AI21 Labs | Microsoft |
| Released | October 2025 | April 2025 |
| Parameters | 3B | 3.8B |
| Architecture | Hybrid: 26 Mamba layers + 2 attention layers | Dense decoder-only transformer (GQA) |
| Context window | 256K tokens (stable up to 1M claimed) | 128K tokens |
| Vocabulary | 64K | 200K |
| License | Apache 2.0 | MIT |
| Q4_K_M GGUF size | 1.93 GB | 2.49 GB |
| Focus | General reasoning, instruction following, long context | Mathematical reasoning |

The parameter counts are close enough that neither model wins on size alone. The architectural difference is the real divide. Phi-4-mini-reasoning is a conventional dense transformer, so every token attends to every previous token and the KV cache grows linearly with context. Jamba Reasoning 3B routes almost everything through Mamba state-space layers, with only 2 of its 28 layers doing full attention. AI21 says this makes the KV cache 8x smaller than an equivalent vanilla transformer.

## Two different definitions of "reasoning"

The word "reasoning" in each model's name means something different, and picking the wrong one for your workload is the main failure mode here.

Phi-4-mini-reasoning is a specialist in the strictest sense. Microsoft trained it in four stages on synthetic math generated by DeepSeek-R1, covering over a million problems from middle school to PhD level. The model card is explicit that it was not evaluated on general downstream tasks. Coding, factual Q&A, summarisation, and multilingual work sit outside the training distribution. Every response begins with a chain-of-thought trace (often thousands of tokens on hard problems), which is useful for verifying derivations and wasteful for everything else.

Jamba Reasoning 3B is a generalist. Its training targeted reasoning broadly (its thinking traces use the same format as DeepSeek-R1), but AI21 positions it for agentic workflows, on-device RAG, and long-document processing rather than competition math. Its benchmark suite reflects that: instruction following and graduate-level knowledge, not AIME.

## Why the benchmark tables don't line up

Here is the awkward part of this comparison: the two vendors report entirely disjoint benchmark suites, and no third-party evaluation covers both models on the same tests. AI21 reports MMLU-Pro, Humanity's Last Exam, and IFBench. Microsoft reports AIME 2024, MATH-500, and GPQA Diamond. You cannot rank them on a shared axis from the published numbers, so anyone who tells you one "beats" the other outright is extrapolating.

What AI21 reports for Jamba Reasoning 3B, against other small general models:

| Benchmark | Jamba Reasoning 3B | Gemma 3 4B | Llama 3.2 3B | Granite 4.0 Micro |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MMLU-Pro | 61.0% | 42.0% | 35.0% | 44.7% |
| Humanity's Last Exam | 6.0% | 5.2% | 5.2% | 5.1% |
| IFBench | 52.0% | 28.0% | 26.0% | 24.8% |

What Microsoft reports for Phi-4-mini-reasoning, against reasoning-tuned models roughly twice its size:

| Benchmark | Phi-4-mini-reasoning (3.8B) | DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B | DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B |
|---|---|---|---|
| AIME 2024 | 57.5% | 53.3% | 43.3% |
| MATH-500 | 94.6% | 91.4% | 86.9% |
| GPQA Diamond | 52.0% | 49.5% | 47.3% |

Read side by side, the tables still tell you something. Phi-4-mini-reasoning's 94.6% on MATH-500 is the highest score we have seen published for any sub-4B model; Microsoft's card shows the base Phi-4-mini at 71.8% on the same test, so the math fine-tune buys about 23 points. Jamba's 52% on IFBench, roughly double the scores of Gemma 3 4B and Llama 3.2 3B, points at unusually reliable multi-step instruction following, which matters more than raw math for agent pipelines. And the fact that AI21 publishes no math benchmarks while Microsoft publishes nothing else is itself informative: each vendor is showing you where its model lives.

## Speed and memory at long context

At short prompts, the two models perform similarly on modest hardware: both Q4 files load in under 4 GB of RAM and generate at usable speeds on CPU. The gap opens as context grows.

Jamba's Mamba-heavy stack processes the sequence as a recurrence, so per-token cost stays close to flat as the prompt lengthens. AI21's launch post claims 40 tokens per second on an M3 MacBook Pro at 32K tokens of context, and 2 to 5x efficiency gains over competing small models. The 256K window is actually reachable on a laptop: budget roughly 6 to 8 GB of RAM at full context with Q4 weights.

Phi-4-mini-reasoning supports 128K tokens on paper, but as a dense transformer its KV cache and attention cost grow with every token. On CPU or a small GPU, long-context prompts get slow well before the architectural limit. In practice this rarely hurts it, because math problems are short. A competition problem fits in a few hundred tokens; the model's output, which can run several thousand tokens of chain-of-thought on hard problems, dominates the compute, not the prompt.

So the long-context question answers itself by workload. Feeding in a 200-page contract: Jamba. Solving problem sets: Phi, where the context window barely matters.

## Running both locally

Both models are a one-line pull. Phi-4-mini-reasoning has a native Ollama entry:

```bash
ollama pull phi4-mini-reasoning
```

Jamba Reasoning 3B runs straight from AI21's GGUF repo:

```bash
ollama run hf.co/ai21labs/AI21-Jamba-Reasoning-3B-GGUF:Q4_K_M
```

Two settings differ enough to trip you up. Jamba wants temperature 0.6 and around 4,096 max new tokens; Phi wants temperature 0.8 with top-p 0.95, and Microsoft recommends up to 32,768 max new tokens because its reasoning traces run long. If you cap Phi's output too low it will cut off mid-derivation and you'll conclude, wrongly, that the model is broken.

At Q4_K_M, Jamba is the smaller download (1.93 GB vs 2.49 GB) and the lighter memory footprint, which matters on 8 GB machines running a browser alongside. If you are unsure which quantization tier to grab, our [GGUF quantization guide](/posts/gguf-quantization-levels-q4-q5-q8/) covers what each level costs in quality, and the [RAM sizing guide](/posts/how-much-ram-for-local-llms/) covers how much headroom to leave.

## Which small reasoning model should you pick?

### Which is better for math?

Phi-4-mini-reasoning, and it is not close. 94.6% on MATH-500 and 57.5% on AIME 2024 are numbers that beat reasoning models twice its size, and AI21 does not publish math scores for Jamba at all. If your workload is tutoring, worked solutions, symbolic checking, or anything where the output is a derivation, Phi is the pick.

### Which is better for long documents and agents?

Jamba Reasoning 3B. The 256K context window is genuinely usable on consumer hardware because the hybrid architecture keeps memory and speed manageable as the prompt grows, and its IFBench score suggests it follows multi-step instructions more reliably than other small models. For on-device RAG, document Q&A, and agent loops that accumulate context, Jamba is the pick.

### Can both run on 8 GB of RAM?

Yes. Both Q4_K_M files need roughly 3 to 4 GB of RAM at normal context lengths, which leaves room for the OS on an 8 GB machine. The exception is Jamba at very long context: a six-figure token count pushes total usage toward 8 GB even with the efficient cache, so treat 16 GB as the comfortable floor for full 256K work.

### Is there any reason to run both?

Actually yes, and at under 4.5 GB combined it is cheap to do. A small router that sends math to Phi and everything else to Jamba covers more ground than any single sub-4B model currently can. Specialists at this scale outperform generalists inside their lane by wide margins, and these two lanes barely overlap.

## Verdict

This comparison has an unusually clean answer because the models barely compete. Choose by workload: Phi-4-mini-reasoning if the task is math, Jamba Reasoning 3B for everything else that needs reasoning in a small footprint, especially anything long-context. If you can spare the disk, keep both.
